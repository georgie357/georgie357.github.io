Hercules and Guilt: What the Myths Never Say Directly

On the twelve labours, what they were actually for, and what it costs to be remade task by task into the thing a god requires

← Back to the blog

The twelve labours of Heracles are among the most famous stories in Western literature, which is to say they are widely known in outline and rarely examined in detail, which is where the interesting parts live. Everyone knows the Nemean lion. Everyone knows the Augean stables. The hydra, the Erymanthian boar, the girdle of Hippolyta — these are the inventory of a man who spent twelve years performing spectacular acts of violence at the direction of a king he despised, and they are told, more often than not, as a sequence of exciting adventures without much attention paid to the fact that the reason he was performing them was that he had killed his own children.

This is the detail the myth handles with considerable delicacy, or possibly considerable evasiveness, depending on your perspective. Heracles — the Romans would call him Hercules, and the name has stuck in the way that Roman renamings tend to stick, with the cheerful persistence of something that does not realize it has displaced something else — was the son of Zeus and Alcmene, a Theban woman of exceptional quality and considerable misfortune, the misfortune being primarily that Zeus had taken an interest in her. This was not, by the standards of Greek mythology, an unusual situation. Zeus's attentions were as predictable as weather and roughly as welcome to the recipients, and Hera's response to them was equally predictable: she transferred her anger downward, to the children, with an efficiency that the gods, who had invented the concept of collateral damage, found entirely reasonable.

The Cradle and What It Means That He Won

Hera began early. When Heracles was an infant — eight months old, in most accounts — she sent two serpents into his crib to kill him. He strangled them. This is one of the most famous images in all of mythology: the baby with a snake in each hand, looking, by all accounts, perfectly calm about the situation. It is usually presented as evidence of his extraordinary strength, which it is. It is also, if you think about it for a moment, a slightly alarming image — the eight-month-old who responds to mortal threat not with distress but with efficient, quiet competence. He was always going to be this way. The question that the myth spends the rest of his life asking is what you do with a man who is constitutionally incapable of not being the most dangerous thing in any room he enters.

He grew up in Thebes. He was educated by a sequence of distinguished teachers, including Chiron the centaur in some versions, and he excelled at everything, which is the kind of biographical detail that sounds like praise and functions as a warning. He married Megara, daughter of the Theban king Creon. He had children. By most accounts, this was the period of his life in which he was, by the modest standards available to demigods with anger-management complications engineered by a jealous goddess, happy.

Then Hera sent the madness.

The sources differ on the details, in the way that ancient sources differ on details they find uncomfortable, which is to say the differences cluster precisely around the most painful parts. In Euripides' Heracles, the madness comes after the labours — Hera waits until the moment of his greatest triumph to destroy him, which is the kind of timing that the gods, who invented irony as a recreational activity, particularly enjoyed. In most other sources, the madness comes before. He kills his children. In some versions, he kills Megara too; in others, she survives. The myth is consistent about the outcome: he wakes from the madness to find that he has done the thing that cannot be undone, and that the hands responsible are his own.

What Penance Is For, and Whether It Works

He goes to Delphi. The Oracle tells him to serve Eurystheus, king of Tiryns, for twelve years, and to perform whatever labours Eurystheus demands. If he does this, he will be purified of the blood guilt and will earn immortality. This is the divine bargain at the centre of the entire myth, and it is worth pausing on it, because it raises a question that Greek mythology does not always answer directly: what, precisely, is the penance for?

He did not choose to kill his children. The madness was Hera's instrument, imposed from outside. By any reasonable modern framework of moral responsibility — intention, agency, the capacity to do otherwise — he bears no guilt for it at all. And yet he carries it. He performs the labours not because the law requires it but because he requires it of himself, which is a distinction the myth draws carefully and which tells you something important about how the Greeks understood guilt: not as a legal status but as a condition, something that adheres to a person regardless of their intentions, something that must be worked off through the body because the mind alone cannot carry it.

The labours themselves are extraordinary, and I will not recount all twelve here because they are well-documented and because the sequence of them is less interesting than the structure of what they mean. Three are worth noting. The Augean stables — cleaning thirty years of accumulated dung from the stables of three thousand cattle by diverting two rivers through them — is the labour the myth uses to humiliate him, to ensure that the penance includes not just danger but indignity, which is the element that distinguishes genuine penance from mere adventure. The descent to the underworld to fetch Cerberus is the labour that takes him furthest from the living world, which is appropriate for a man who has himself been in a kind of underworld since the morning he woke from the madness. And the golden apples of the Hesperides is the labour the myth associates most clearly with what lies beyond the labours — the world's edge, the garden at the rim of things, the hint of something that might, eventually, be rest.

The end is not rest. The centaur Nessus, dying from a wound Heracles gave him, told Deianira — Heracles' wife, who had watched him his whole life and loved him with the specific exhausted devotion of someone who loves a force of nature — that his blood was a love charm. She believed him. When Heracles seemed to be drifting toward another woman, she sent him a robe soaked in the blood, which was not a love charm but a contact poison derived from the hydra's venom. He put on the robe. He could not take it off. He built his own funeral pyre on Mount Oeta, climbed onto it, and asked his companions to light it.

He was taken to Olympus. He was deified. He married Hebe, goddess of youth. The myth gives him, finally, everything it had spent his entire life denying him — peace, stability, a home that would not be taken from him. It is the kind of ending that sits uneasily with everything that preceded it, which is perhaps the point. The labours did not resolve the guilt. They transformed it into something that a god could carry. That is not the same thing as forgiveness. It is, possibly, the closest the Greek universe gets to it.
