What Is the Iliad Actually About?

On Homer's poem, its strange twenty-three-day structure, and the question of what it means when the best warrior stops fighting

← Back to the blog

The first thing to know about the Iliad is that it is not a story about the Trojan War. This is not a semantic quibble. The Trojan War lasted ten years. The Iliad covers twenty-three days. It begins in year nine, and it ends before Troy falls — before the horse, before the fire, before the long procession of captive women to the ships. Homer tells you none of that. He stops at a funeral, which is either a strange structural choice or an extraordinarily deliberate one, and the answer, as it turns out, is the latter.

If you came to the poem expecting a complete account of the war — the abduction of Helen, the mustering of the fleet at Aulis, Iphigenia sacrificed so the winds would blow, ten years of fighting compressed into a satisfying narrative arc ending with the city in flames — you would be disappointed within the first twenty lines. The poem does not begin with Helen. It begins with a word.

The word is menis. Rage. It is the first word of the poem, placed there with the deliberateness of a mason setting a cornerstone, and it tells you exactly what you are about to read: not a war story, but a story about what happens when one man's rage is withdrawn from a war, and what that costs everyone around him.

The Quarrel That Starts Everything

Achilles is the greatest warrior on the Greek side — this is not disputed by anyone in the poem, including the Trojans, who are not in the habit of flattering their enemies. He is also, by a considerable margin, the most difficult person in the Greek camp, which is saying something given that Agamemnon is also present. The quarrel between them begins over a woman named Chryseis, whom Agamemnon has taken as a prize of war and refuses to return to her father, a priest of Apollo, despite the priest's entreaties and the god's increasingly pointed objections (Apollo sends a plague; Agamemnon remains unpersuaded until the body count becomes impossible to ignore). When Agamemnon finally agrees to give Chryseis back, he announces that he will take Briseis — Achilles' prize — as compensation. It is the kind of decision that makes strategic sense only if you have decided that your own dignity is more important than winning the war, which is, in fact, precisely what Agamemnon has decided.

Achilles' response is to stop fighting. He withdraws to his tent, takes his Myrmidons with him, and refuses to engage. This sounds, in summary, petulant. In context it is something considerably more interesting, because the poem immediately demonstrates what the Greek army is without him: comprehensively, catastrophically outmatched. The Trojans, led by Hector, push the Greeks back to their ships. People die in large numbers. The situation deteriorates with the patient efficiency of a structural problem that was always there and simply required someone to remove the load-bearing column to make itself visible.

The philosophical problem at the centre of Achilles' withdrawal — and the Iliad is, beneath its bronze and blood, a deeply philosophical poem — is this: what is the correct response when the system that claims your loyalty treats you with contempt? Achilles has an answer. It is not a comfortable one, and the poem does not pretend it is. His rage is understandable. Its consequences are not his alone to bear. That gap between the justice of a feeling and the cost of acting on it is where most of the poem lives.

Patroclus, and the Price of Staying in the Tent

Eventually, because the situation has become untenable, Patroclus — Achilles' closest companion, which the ancient sources treat as a fact requiring no elaboration and which readers have been elaborating upon ever since — asks permission to enter the battle wearing Achilles' armour. The idea is that the sight of the armour will hearten the Greeks and unsettle the Trojans, who know perfectly well what it means when Achilles is on the field. Achilles agrees, with the condition that Patroclus not push too far. Patroclus, because the Iliad is a tragedy and tragedies require their characters to do exactly the thing they were told not to do, pushes too far. He is killed by Hector, who strips the armour from the body.

What follows is Achilles' return to the war — not because he has forgiven Agamemnon, not because the political situation has been resolved, but because Patroclus is dead and someone must account for that. He returns with a grief so absolute that it has burned away the original quarrel entirely, leaving only the clean fact of what he intends to do. He kills Hector. He drags the body behind his chariot around the walls of Troy, for twelve days, because grief at its most extreme can look very much like an argument with a corpse about whether the universe is just.

And then Priam comes to him. The old king of Troy, Hector's father, comes alone at night to the tent of the man who killed his son, and asks for the body back. It is one of the great scenes in world literature — not because it resolves anything, not because it brings either man peace, but because it does something considerably rarer: it makes two enemies recognize each other as human beings, briefly, without that recognition changing anything about the war.

The poem ends with Hector's funeral. Troy is still standing. The war is still going. Homer stops here.

Why Homer Doesn't Show Troy Falling

This is the structural decision that confuses readers expecting a complete war narrative and rewards everyone else. The Iliad is not about the fall of Troy because the fall of Troy is not what the poem is about. The fall of Troy is the backdrop, the context, the thing that will happen eventually and that everyone in the poem already knows is coming (several characters, including Achilles, know perfectly well they are going to die before it does). What the poem is about — what menis, that first word, announces — is the human cost of rage, pride, and the specific kind of grief that arises when someone realizes too late what their choices have cost other people.

A chronicle of the full war would have dissipated this. The tight structure — twenty-three days, one quarrel, one death, one return, one funeral — holds the poem's argument together in a way that ten years never could. The walls of Troy are the frame; what matters is what happens in front of them.

The Aethiopis, a poem now mostly lost, picks up where Homer stops — after Hector's funeral, as the war resumes and new allies arrive to fight for Troy. That is where Penthesilea comes: Queen of the Amazons, twelve warriors at her back, arriving in the war's final weeks when the outcome is already written and she comes anyway. But that is a different story, and Homer, who had impeccable instincts about where stories end, wisely left it to someone else.
