The Amazons: What Greek Mythology Got Wrong

On the Amazon myths, the archaeological evidence, and why Greek heroes kept having to fight them

← Back to the blog

Every major Greek hero fights the Amazons. This is not coincidence. Heracles' ninth labour involves travelling to the shores of the Black Sea to retrieve the golden girdle of Hippolyta, the Amazon queen — a mission that, depending on the version of the myth one reads, ends either in negotiation interrupted by divine interference or in straightforward violence, since Hera, who had opinions about Heracles accomplishing things, disguised herself as an Amazon warrior and stirred the others into a fight. Theseus, in another branch of the tradition, abducts an Amazon queen named Antiope (or Hippolyta, in the versions where Hippolyta is not already dead, which is the kind of timeline inconsistency one encounters regularly in Greek mythology and learns to navigate with charity). Bellerophon, in Lycia, fights them from the back of Pegasus. Achilles kills their queen Penthesilea on the plain of Troy. The pattern is too consistent to be accidental, and what it reveals is less about the Amazons themselves than about the Greek mythological imagination and what it needed them to be.

What it needed them to be was a worthy opponent that a hero could defeat without it reflecting badly on anyone. Fighting another Greek city-state was politics; fighting monsters was either unambiguous heroism or (if the monster was sympathetic enough) tragedy. The Amazons occupied a useful middle ground — human enough to require real combat skill, foreign enough to be disposable, female enough to carry a specific charge of transgression that Greek culture found productively threatening. They were opponents whose defeat meant something beyond the mere accumulation of victories.

Where the Maps Become Uncertain

In the mythological geography, the Amazons live at the edge of the known world. Their city, Themiscyra, sits on the Thermodon river near the southern coast of the Black Sea — modern Turkey, in the region the Greeks called Pontus. This placement is significant: the Amazons are consistently located just beyond where Greek geographical knowledge becomes reliable, in the zone where the maps begin to say things like here be barbarians and the coastal trade routes give way to rumour. The mythological Amazons are neighbours of Scythia — the vast steppe culture that occupied the region north of the Black Sea — and this proximity matters, because it is Scythia where the archaeology becomes interesting.

Adrienne Mayor, in The Amazons: Lives and Legends of Warrior Women Across the Ancient World, makes the case at considerable length that the Amazon myths are not pure invention but a Greek response to genuine reports of Scythian and other steppe cultures where women participated in warfare. The evidence for this is archaeological and has been accumulating since the Soviet era: graves in the steppe region, dating from the period corresponding to the mythological Bronze Age and later, containing women buried with weapons — short swords, bows, quivers of arrows, horse-riding equipment. In some cases the skeletal evidence shows injuries consistent with combat. The women were buried the way warriors were buried, because they were warriors, and the Greek traders and travellers who encountered these cultures and brought back stories to the Aegean world were probably not entirely fabricating what they reported.

The distance between the archaeological Scythian warrior-women and the mythological Amazons is, however, considerable, and it is worth being precise about what the myths changed. The mythological Amazons are a separatist society — a nation organised around the complete exclusion of men, in which daughters are raised and sons are either sent away, killed, or (in the most dramatic versions) blinded and lamed and kept as domestic labourers, which is either savage irony or wish-fulfilment, depending on one's perspective, and which the ancient sources do not appear to have decided. The archaeology does not support this. What the archaeology supports is something considerably less dramatic: a culture where women rode horses, used weapons, and in some cases fought alongside the men of their society in a way that Greek culture, which had strict views about the appropriate activities of women, found bizarre enough to require mythological processing into a cautionary extreme.

The Amazons of myth remove men from the equation entirely. The Scythian warrior-women of archaeology appear to have operated within mixed societies. These are different things, and the distinction matters because it shows what the Greek mythological imagination did with the raw material of genuine cultural difference: it amplified it to the point of useful absurdity, producing a version of female martial power so comprehensive and so threatening that defeating it constituted a civilisational achievement. You do not just kill one female warrior when you defeat the Amazons; you symbolically restore the proper order of the world.

Why They Had to Keep Losing

The question of why Greek heroes had to keep defeating the Amazons — why the same battle had to be fought by Heracles, by Theseus, by Bellerophon, by Achilles, in every generation — is answered by the mythological logic itself. The Amazons, in the Greek tradition, are not defeated permanently. There are always more of them. They rebuild. They send another queen. The threat they represent is not eliminated by any individual victory, because what they represent is not a people but an idea, and ideas are not defeated by killing a queen.

Penthesilea is the last and in many ways the most complicated figure in this sequence. She arrives at Troy after every other Amazon confrontation has already been resolved, after the tradition has already established that this is how these encounters end, and she comes anyway — she comes knowing, the myth implies, how it ends, carrying the blood-guilt from her sister's death and the need to do something with it, to transform grief into action before it simply consumed her. She fights brilliantly. She loses.

What the myth adds to the formula, in Penthesilea's case, is the moment after — Achilles removing the helmet, seeing the face, the look that passes between the greatest warrior alive and the woman he has just killed, which the ancient world did not know quite what to call and kept returning to on their vases anyway, drawn back repeatedly to an image they could not explain and could not leave alone. This is the Amazon myth at its most honest: not the defeat that confirms the proper order of things, but the recognition — arriving too late, as recognitions in tragedy always do — that the categories the myth had been relying on were not quite adequate to the people inside them.
