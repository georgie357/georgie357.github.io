Pegasus: The Horse That Was Never Just a Horse

On the winged horse's origins, his trajectory through the myth, and what happens to heroes' tools when the heroes are done with them

← Back to the blog

The birth of Pegasus is one of the stranger moments in Greek mythology, which is a field with considerable competition for that distinction. Perseus cuts off the head of Medusa — the snake-haired Gorgon whose gaze turns men to stone, a figure of such concentrated mythological dread that Athena wore her face on her breastplate as a weapon — and from the stump of Medusa's neck spring two things: Chrysaor, a giant carrying a golden sword, and Pegasus, a winged horse. This is not how horses are normally produced, and the myth does not pause to explain it, in the same way a person who has just watched something extraordinary might neglect to mention that it was also, technically, impossible. Both emerge fully formed. Chrysaor largely disappears from the tradition shortly afterwards. Pegasus has a considerably better-documented career.

The name, in the ancient sources, is connected to the Greek word for springs — pegai — which is appropriate, since springs appear wherever Pegasus touches the earth. The most famous of these is the Hippocrene, the Horse's Spring, which opened on Mount Helicon when the winged horse struck the rock with his hoof. The Hippocrene became sacred to the Muses, the nine daughters of Zeus and Mnemosyne who presided over poetry, music, history, astronomy, and the various other activities that the ancient world classified as art rather than labour. This association — a horse born from a monster's severed neck, sacred to the inspirers of poetry — is either a paradox or a very compressed argument about where artistic inspiration actually comes from, depending on how generous you are feeling about Greek mythological logic.

The Bridle and the Spring at Corinth

The story that matters most for Pegasus is not his birth but his taming, and it begins at Corinth — a city that had planted itself like a toll-keeper at the only viable land crossing between northern and southern Greece, and which consequently accumulated both considerable wealth and a certain civic self-satisfaction that the rest of Greece found mildly irritating. It was here that Bellerophon, a young man of good family and better instincts, came to find the horse. The Pirene spring at Corinth was where Pegasus drank, and Bellerophon had been told — either by the prophet Polyidus, or in a dream, depending on which version of the myth one trusts, and the ancient sources are not in close agreement on this point — that he needed a golden bridle.

In some versions, Athena provides the bridle directly. In others, Bellerophon wakes from his dream and finds it beside him, which raises questions the myth declines to answer about divine logistics. Either way: he goes to the Pirene spring. He finds the horse. He approaches with the golden bridle. Pegasus — who is, after all, a creature born from divine violence and associated with the inspirers of art rather than the inspirers of obedience — accepts it. The image of Bellerophon bridling Pegasus appears on more ancient Greek coins than almost any other mythological scene, which tells you something about what the ancient world considered a satisfying image of human capacity. The moment a man puts a bridle on something divine and it holds — that is the moment the myth considers worth commemorating in metal.

What Pegasus enables is straightforward enough, if you accept the premise: Bellerophon flies. He kills the Chimera — the fire-breathing composite monster of lion, goat, and serpent that is either a supernatural horror or, in the more archaeological interpretation, a memory of volcanic activity in Lycia; the myth is comfortable with both readings simultaneously. He defeats the Amazons. He fights the Solymi. He accomplishes, from the air, things that could not be accomplished on the ground. Pegasus is, in practical terms, a military technology of decisive superiority, and the myth treats him accordingly — as a tool that makes the impossible merely difficult. The horse's own experience of these campaigns is not recorded.

Olympus, a Gadfly, and the Thunderbolts

The fatal attempt changes everything, and it is worth being precise about what happens. Bellerophon, having accomplished things that no mortal had accomplished, decides — and this is the decision that defines the myth's argument about the difference between achievement and hubris — that he will fly to Olympus. Not to visit. To stay. He aims Pegasus at the home of the gods and the horse goes, because the horse always goes where he is aimed, and this is perhaps the saddest thing the myth records: the instrument of a man's ambition cannot prevent its own use.

Zeus sends a gadfly. The gadfly stings Pegasus. The horse bucks — involuntarily, instinctively, the way any living creature responds to sudden pain, with no malice and no intention and no particular interest in the theological implications of the moment — and Bellerophon falls. He survives, which is unusual for men who have attempted Olympus, but he spends the rest of his life wandering the plain of Aleion, alone, lame, avoided by everyone, eating his heart out over what he lost, which is the myth's version of a fate worse than death. The horse, meanwhile, continues upward.

Pegasus reaches Olympus. He enters Zeus's service. His specific function, from this point, is to carry the thunderbolts. This is simultaneously the most powerful job in the cosmos — you are the delivery mechanism for divine wrath itself, for the weapon with which Zeus ended the war of the Titans and maintains the current cosmic order — and somehow, given everything that came before, a little undignified. The creature born from Medusa's blood, sacred to the Muses, associated with the springs of poetic inspiration, who enabled a mortal to accomplish the impossible, ends his mythological career as a courier. He is very good at it. He is never mentioned dissatisfied. Whether this constitutes a good ending depends entirely on what you think the story was ever about.

And this brings us to the thing worth saying about Pegasus, which is that he is the most portable metaphor in Greek mythology — more portable even than Prometheus's fire, more portable than the apple of discord — because the image of the winged horse is not attached to any specific meaning. He is ascent. He is the means by which a man exceeds the natural limits of his condition. He is inspiration. He is the vehicle of hubris. He is divine power in harness, unglamorous work done with extraordinary capability. He gets attached to whatever story needs the image of going higher, and this is not a criticism of the myth but an observation about how myths use images: a symbol that means too many specific things eventually means nothing; a symbol flexible enough to carry any story goes everywhere. Pegasus went everywhere. He is still going.
