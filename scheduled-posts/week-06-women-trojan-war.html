The Women the Trojan War Forgot

On Briseis, Chryseis, Cassandra, and the women whose names appear in the margins of the Trojan War

← Back to the blog

There is a useful thought experiment available to anyone reading the Iliad carefully: count how many named women appear in the poem and then ask yourself how many of them make a decision that changes the story. The answer, which the poem reaches with the blithe efficiency of something that does not realize it is making a point, is roughly none. This is not an accident of composition and it is not simply a product of the era (Euripides, writing a century and a half later, managed to give the same women considerably more interiority, which tells you something about what is a structural limitation and what is a choice). It is, rather, how the war was told — as a story about men, in which women appear as objects of dispute, recipients of suffering, and, on rare occasions, loyal witnesses to catastrophe.

What follows is an attempt to give several of them back their details. The sources are incomplete and, in some cases, contradictory. The gaps are significant. But the gaps are themselves interesting, because what a literary tradition chooses not to record is often as revealing as what it keeps.

Briseis and Chryseis: The Women the War Was Not Actually About

Chryseis appears in the Iliad for approximately the length of time it takes Agamemnon to refuse to return her and Apollo to respond with a plague. She is the daughter of Chryses, a priest of Apollo, taken as a prize when the Greeks sacked a nearby city. Her father comes to ransom her. Agamemnon sends him away. Apollo sends the plague. Agamemnon eventually, under considerable pressure, gives her back. She is not consulted at any stage of this process. Her name means, more or less, "golden girl," which is either a coincidence or a summary of how the poem regards her function in events.

Briseis is more interesting, though the Iliad does not always behave as if it knows this. She too was taken in a raid — on Lyrnessus, a city under Trojan protection — and her husband and three brothers were killed in the attack, by Achilles personally, which is the kind of biographical detail that the poem records and then appears to forget. She is given to Achilles as a prize. Agamemnon takes her. Achilles stops fighting. She is, in the logic of the poem, the cause of the quarrel that structures the entire narrative — and yet we learn almost nothing about what she thinks of any of it.

Ovid, writing several centuries later, gives her a voice in the Heroides — a letter from Briseis to Achilles, asking why he has not come for her, insisting that she chose him, that she wept when she was led away, that she is not simply a grievance but a person with a specific and costly attachment to a specific man. It is, by any fair reading, more than Homer gave her. Whether it is accurate is a different question, since Ovid's Briseis is also Ovid's Briseis, shaped by a Roman poet's interest in the psychology of the abandoned woman. But it is at least a corrective gesture — the acknowledgment that someone, somewhere, noticed the omission.

Cassandra and Andromache: Knowing Everything and Being Heard by No One

Cassandra is the most formally tragic figure in the entire Trojan War cycle, and she is tragic in a very particular and uncomfortable way: she is right. She was given the gift of prophecy by Apollo and the curse of disbelief when she refused him. Everything she predicts comes true. She predicted that Paris's return from Sparta would destroy Troy. She predicted the fall of the city. She predicted, at the end, what would happen to her personally — taken by Agamemnon, killed by Clytemnestra — and she walked into it anyway, because knowing the future does not give you the option of exiting it.

The details of her story after Troy falls are not pleasant reading. She was assaulted by Ajax the Lesser in Athena's own temple during the sack of the city, an act of sacrilege so severe that the goddess who had been helping the Greeks promptly switched sides, which suggests that even the divine patrons of armies have limits. She was then given to Agamemnon as a prize — Cassandra, who had been a princess, a prophetess, and was still by any account a person — and taken to Mycenae, where Clytemnestra killed them both. She knew this would happen. She said so, in Aeschylus, with a directness that borders on the conversational. The Chorus did not believe her, because the Chorus never does, and she walked inside anyway.

Andromache, Hector's wife, is the war's most efficient portrait of anticipatory grief. Her great scene in the Iliad — on the walls, watching Hector march out, holding their infant son Astyanax who is frightened by his father's helmet — contains the whole of the war's human cost in a domestic moment that Homer understood perfectly well was doing the work of an entire elegy. She knows Hector will die. Hector knows Hector will die. They speak to each other with the careful precision of people who are aware they are running out of time and have agreed not to say so directly. After Troy falls, she is given to Neoptolemus, Achilles' son, which is the kind of detail that seems designed to be maximally devastating.

Helen is a more complicated case, and she deserves a longer treatment than a paragraph can provide. The "face that launched a thousand ships" version of her story — passive, beautiful, cause of everything, accountable for nothing — is not really what the ancient sources support if you read them carefully. She has opinions. She expresses them. In the Iliad she is contemptuous of Paris and clear-eyed about what her presence in Troy has cost the people around her. But her story, too, belongs elsewhere.

Hecuba is different from all of them, and she is different in a specific way: she acts. The sources — Euripides above all — are unambiguous about what she does and why she does it. She does not wait. She does not watch. She plans, she executes, and the myth records it plainly, without the flinching that tends to accompany accounts of women doing things that the narrative did not leave them room to do. She is, in that sense, the exception that demonstrates the rule — which tells you something about what history considers worth remembering, and what it prefers to treat as background.

The Hound of Troy: The Vengeance of Hecuba

The story of the Trojan War told entirely from Hecuba's perspective — the woman who ran the city, buried the sons, and still found a way to act when everything else was gone.
