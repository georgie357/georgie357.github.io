Where to Start With Greek Mythology Retellings

A map of the current landscape — who is doing what, what each approach offers, and how to find the right entry point for you

← Back to the blog

There are more Greek mythology retellings being published now than at any point since the Renaissance, which is simultaneously wonderful and slightly overwhelming, in the way that any genuine abundance of good things tends to be. The myths are inexhaustible — two and a half thousand years of storytelling have not come close to exhausting them — and contemporary writers have discovered, or perhaps rediscovered, that the original material rewards serious literary attention rather than merely the narrative equivalent of dressing up in a toga. The question is not whether to read Greek mythology retellings. The question is which one to read first, because the answer to that depends entirely on what you are actually looking for, and the field is broad enough that the wrong entry point can send a reader in entirely the wrong direction.

So: a map. This is not a ranked list (ranked lists of books are a minor intellectual crime, since they imply that literature is a competition with a winner, which it is not), but an honest account of what different approaches offer and who they are for.

A Taxonomy of Approaches

If you want emotional devastation about the Trojan War: Madeline Miller's The Song of Achilles is the obvious starting point and it deserves its reputation. It is first-person, intimate, written in a voice of extraordinary precision and feeling, and it will rearrange something in your chest that will not go back to where it was. Miller's great insight is that the Iliad's most interesting emotional story is not the war but the relationship at the centre of it, and she pursues that insight to its logical conclusion without flinching. Her Circe is equally good, differently good — more expansive, more interested in power and selfhood than in grief, and if anything more ambitious in its scope.

If you want the women's perspectives on the Trojan War: Pat Barker's The Silence of the Girls and its sequel The Women of Troy are the place to go, and they are doing something different from Miller — less interested in beauty, more interested in the grinding reality of what it meant to be a woman on the wrong side of that war, which is to say a woman without status, without protection, and without the kind of narrative dignity the Iliad reserved for its heroes. Natalie Haynes's A Thousand Ships covers similar territory with more voices and considerably more mordant wit; it is structured as a series of portraits, each a woman the war touched, and the cumulative effect is powerful in a way that no single portrait could be. Haynes, it should be said, is one of the wittiest writers currently working in this space, and her wit is not a lightening of the subject but a way of seeing it clearly.

If you want accessible mythology with personality: Stephen Fry's Mythos and its sequels (Heroes, Troy) are the entry point for readers who want the full sweep of Greek mythology without the commitment of primary sources or the density of literary fiction. Fry is warm, erudite, funny, and genuinely knowledgeable, and his version of the myths is a pleasure to read in the way that sitting across from a very well-read and enthusiastic friend is a pleasure. He makes no pretensions to literary fiction; he is retelling the myths as stories, and he does it very well.

If you want Greek tragedy with contemporary emotional weight: Colm Tóibín's House of Names, which retells the Oresteia, is one of the most formally accomplished Greek mythology retellings in recent decades — spare, cold, written in a prose of such deliberate restraint that the violence, when it comes, lands like a stone dropped from a height. It is not for readers who want warmth, but it is for readers who want to understand why the Greeks thought tragedy was a form of civic instruction.

If you want mythology told by a narrator who has opinions about everything: I would suggest the Myths of the Ancient World series, starting with The Fall from Heaven: The Myth of Bellerophon and Pegasus — not because it is the most important myth in the tradition but because it establishes the voice that carries all four books, and the voice is the thing. The narrator is omniscient, historically self-aware, given to long parenthetical asides that do genuine analytical work and to philosophical digressions that are not padding but argument, and has a particular interest in the gap between what the poets remembered and what the experience actually was. From Bellerophon the order runs: Hercules and the Cradle of Thunder, then The Hound of Troy: The Vengeance of Hecuba, then The Amazon's End: The Tragedy of Penthesilea. They can be read in any order — each is self-contained — but the narrator accumulates resonance across the series, and reading from the beginning rewards you for it.

Starting With the Primary Sources

There is an argument, which I find genuinely compelling, that the best place to start with Greek mythology is not a retelling at all but a translation — specifically, a translation of Homer. The Iliad and the Odyssey are not difficult in the way that readers sometimes assume they will be. They are long, yes, and they have passages of catalogue and description that require patience, but the central emotional intelligence of both poems is immediately accessible to anyone who has ever lost something, wanted something, or been away from home longer than intended, which is most people. The question is only which translation.

Robert Fagles' translations of both poems remain the gold standard for a certain kind of reading experience — high-energy, muscular, designed to convey the forward momentum of oral performance. Emily Wilson's translation of the Odyssey is a revelation: scrupulously accurate, written in a contemporary English that clarifies rather than archaizes, and deeply attentive to the things the poem says about power and servitude that earlier translations tended to soften. For the Iliad, Caroline Alexander's translation is excellent, particularly for readers interested in the war rather than the heroism. Euripides — who is the primary source for Hecuba, Medea, and much of the Trojan Women tradition — is more immediately readable than his reputation suggests; the Penguin Classics editions are a reasonable starting point, and his plays are genuinely short.

What the best retellings share, whether they are Miller's emotional precision or Barker's realism or Haynes's wit or the omniscient-narrator approach — is a refusal to treat the myths as adventure stories with the psychology removed. The myths survived two and a half thousand years not because they are exciting plots but because they are documents of human experience: documents of what it costs to be ambitious, to be a woman in a world structured around men, to love someone mortal, to win a war and discover that winning was not the point. The retellings that last are the ones that understand this, that take the mythology seriously as a way of thinking about being alive rather than as source material to be dressed up in contemporary clothes. There are, right now, enough of those retellings to keep a reader occupied for years. This is a good time to start.
