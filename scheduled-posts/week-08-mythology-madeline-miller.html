Greek Mythology Books for Fans of Madeline Miller

Where to go after The Song of Achilles and Circe — mythology told with literary seriousness

← Back to the blog

Madeline Miller did something that is considerably harder than it looks: she made Greek mythology feel emotionally urgent to readers who had not previously thought it was any of their business. The Song of Achilles in particular managed to take a story everyone knew the ending of — Achilles dies at Troy, this is not a spoiler, Homer mentions it within the first hundred lines of the Iliad — and make it devastating anyway, which is the specific achievement of good tragic fiction: the destination is known; the journey undoes you regardless. The trick she used, and it is a good trick, was the secondary perspective. Patroclus, who in Homer is present and significant but not the point of view, becomes the lens through which Achilles is seen — which means we see him the way someone in love sees a person, which is to say, from very close, with great attention, and with a particular interest in the moments when the public legend and the private person diverge.

Circe did something related but different: it took a character who appears in the Odyssey as an obstacle — a sorceress who turns men into pigs, is outwitted by Odysseus, and sends him on his way — and asked what her life looked like from the inside, across the full span of the centuries she lived it. The result is a novel about power and its cost, about what it means to be competent in a world that would prefer you helpless, and about the slow, grinding work of becoming yourself when the self in question is inconvenient to everyone around you. Both books are, at bottom, about people the canonical versions of the stories do not have much use for.

If you have read both and are looking for what to read next, here is an honest guide to the options, organized by what they do well and who they are for.

For Readers Who Want More Trojan War Women

Pat Barker's The Silence of the Girls and The Women of Troy are the most direct companions to Miller's work available, in the sense that they are explicitly responding to Homer and explicitly interested in what the women in the camp experienced while the men were busy being epic. Briseis is the narrator — which is the correct instinct, since her position is formally impossible: a prize of war who is also, by the poem's own account, a person with history, grief, and opinions — and Barker handles it with the dry, clear-eyed precision of a novelist who has spent decades writing about people in extremis. The register is different from Miller's: less lush, more austere. If Miller is warm, Barker is exact. Both are serious. If you want to stay in the Trojan War and hear from the women who were there, start with The Silence of the Girls.

Natalie Haynes' A Thousand Ships takes a different structural approach: many voices, many women, cycling through the full cast of the Trojan War from multiple vantage points. Penelope waiting in Ithaca. Hecuba on the shore of Thrace. Calliope, the Muse, narrating the whole thing with the slightly exasperated competence of someone who has been asked to write an epic about the men when the interesting stories are clearly elsewhere. It is funny, which is not always what readers expect from Trojan War fiction, and it is generous — there is real affection in Haynes' treatment of even the minor figures. Readers who want breadth rather than depth should go here.

Colm Tóibín's House of Names is a Clytemnestra novel, which means it begins at the moment Agamemnon sacrifices Iphigenia at Aulis so the fleet will sail, and proceeds from there with the patient accumulation of grievance that characterises both good revenge tragedy and bad marriages. Tóibín's prose is spare to the point of austerity — almost Biblical in its flatness, which is a deliberate choice that creates a particular kind of dread. It is not the warmest book on this list, but it is one of the most controlled, and if you are interested in Clytemnestra specifically, or in how the Trojan War looks from Mycenae, from the palace that is waiting for a man to come home and account for himself, this is the book for it.

For Readers Who Want the Myths Themselves, Told Well

Stephen Fry's Mythos, Heroes, and Troy are in a different register from everything else on this list — they are retellings rather than novels, the myths rendered accessible and often very funny by a narrator who is clearly delighted by the material and wants you to share his enthusiasm. They are not the place to go for psychological depth or literary experiment. They are the place to go if you want to know what actually happens in the myths, told by someone who has read everything and organized it with considerable wit. Think of them as the ideal companion texts to the novels — the reference shelf you consult when the fiction sends you back to the original stories.

The Myths of the Ancient World series — four books, starting with The Fall from Heaven (Bellerophon and Pegasus), continuing through Hercules and the Cradle of Thunder and The Hound of Troy (Hecuba's story), and concluding with The Amazon's End (Penthesilea at Troy) — uses a different approach from Miller's: not first person, not close third, but an omniscient narrator who is openly present on the page, opinionated about what is happening, and interested in the gap between the legend as it was told and the reality as it might have been experienced. The tone is drier than Miller's and the sentences are longer and the humor is darker, which is either a feature or a warning depending on your preferences. Readers who came to Miller from a literary fiction background rather than a fantasy background tend to find the series particularly suited to them. Readers who want the lush emotional interiority of The Song of Achilles may prefer Barker or Haynes. Both are valid preferences. The series exists for the readers who want to feel that the narrator knows considerably more than the characters and has decided to share it with them, parenthetically, in the middle of describing a battle.

The commonality across all of these books — Miller's, Barker's, Haynes', Tóibín's, and the series — is the conviction that the myths contain more than the canonical tellings extracted from them, and that the figures the ancient world treated as secondary or ornamental tend, under sustained literary attention, to turn out to be the most interesting people in the room. This seems, thirty centuries after Homer, like a reasonable thing to have noticed.
