Who Was Penthesilea? The Amazon Queen History Almost Forgot

On the Penthesilea myth, the Aethiopis, and what Achilles felt when he saw her face

← Back to the blog

The name Penthesilea appears in Homer's Iliad exactly once. She is mentioned in the poem's closing lines — a brief, almost parenthetical reference to the Amazon queen who fought at Troy after the Iliad's story ends. Homer calls her "the man-slaying Penthesilea" and notes that she was loved by the Trojans, and then moves on. She does not appear again. The main narrative had no room for her.

The fuller account of Penthesilea's story survived in the Aethiopis — a now-lost poem by Arctinus of Miletus, written in the eighth century BC, that continued the Trojan War narrative beyond Hector's death. We know its contents only through summaries and references in later works, which is itself an interesting fact about how myth survives: in fragments, in other people's footnotes, in the memories of people who had read the original and thought to mention what it contained.

What those summaries tell us is this: Penthesilea was the Queen of the Amazons. She came to Troy to fight for the Trojans, bringing twelve warrior companions with her. She fought with extraordinary courage — enough that the Greeks, facing her, wavered. And then Achilles killed her.

The twist, which Arctinus apparently described in some detail and which later poets found irresistible, is what happened next. When Achilles removed Penthesilea's helmet and saw her face, he was struck — depending on the source — by grief, by guilt, or by something that the ancient world found difficult to categorize. Some versions say he wept. Some say he fell in love. One tradition holds that a Greek soldier named Thersites mocked him for this, and Achilles killed Thersites on the spot, which caused considerable political complications.

Who Was Penthesilea, Actually?

The myths are consistent on certain points. She was a daughter of Ares, the god of war — a lineage that appears in the Amazons' stories repeatedly, suggesting that the myth-makers wanted to be clear that these were not women who had accidentally found themselves fighting, but women who came from a tradition where war was a birthright. She was the sister of Hippolyta, whose belt appears in the ninth of Heracles' labours. She killed Hippolyta by accident, in a hunting expedition, and the resulting blood-guilt was one of the reasons she came to Troy: seeking either absolution or a death in battle that would settle the debt.

The Amazon women in Greek mythology occupy a complicated position in the Greek imagination — a society organized along lines the Greeks found both transgressive and fascinating, existing somewhere at the edge of the known world where the maps became uncertain. The myths consistently send Greek heroes to fight them: Heracles, Theseus, Bellerophon. The heroes consistently win. But the Amazons keep appearing, keep fighting, keep mattering to the story. They are never quite defeated in the way that the myths' logic would seem to require.

Penthesilea is the last Amazon queen in the mythological sequence. She arrives at Troy knowing — the myth implies — that her cause is lost, that the Trojan War is nearly over, that she is twelve women against an army that has been besieging the city for a decade. She comes anyway.

Why Did She Come?

The Greek myths don't always explain motivation in terms we would find immediately recognizable. Characters act for reasons of honour, of blood-debt, of divine instruction. But Penthesilea's decision to come to Troy has a quality that transcends the formal mythological language: she was the last war-queen of a people the world was in the process of forgetting, and this was the last great war, and she chose to be in it.

What Quintus of Smyrna adds in his Posthomerica — the later, fuller retelling that draws on the lost Aethiopis — is detail about the battle itself. Penthesilea fights brilliantly. She kills several named Greek warriors. She drives the Greeks back toward their ships. And then Achilles arrives, and the outcome is what the outcome always is when Achilles arrives, which is one of the narrative's structural facts the myth never pretends otherwise about.

Whether the story of Penthesilea constitutes a tragedy or a kind of terrible integrity depends on how you read the myth. She came knowing she would probably die. She fought as if she might not. The ancient world remembered her for this, and then mostly forgot her — which is, the narrator of The Amazon's End would observe, one of history's more reliable patterns.
