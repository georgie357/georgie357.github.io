The Real Myth of Bellerophon: Why He Fell From Heaven

On hubris, Pegasus, and what the simplified version of the legend leaves out

← Back to the blog

Most people who know the name Bellerophon know two things about him: he rode a winged horse called Pegasus, and he fell. The fall — hurled from the sky by Zeus, landing in a thorny plain called the Aleian Field, blind and lame and alone — is the part that sticks in the memory. The Greeks had a word, hubris, for the particular kind of pride that attracts divine punishment, and Bellerophon is often cited as the exemplary case: the man who won too much, wanted more, and paid for it.

This is not wrong, exactly. But it is incomplete in ways that matter.

What the simplified version of the Bellerophon Greek mythology omits is how much was taken from him before he was given anything at all. He was born in Corinth — a city the ancient world described as planted like a toll-keeper at the only gate between north and south Greece, which tells you something about its character: practical, commercial, not particularly romantic. He was a king's son, which in the ancient world carried specific implications about expectation and inheritance, and then he was effectively exiled, because a queen made advances he refused and then told her husband a different story about what had happened. This is a narrative the Greeks told fairly often, which suggests it reflected something genuine about the dynamics of power and accusation in the ancient world. The result was that Bellerophon found himself carrying a sealed letter to a foreign king — a letter that, had he been able to read it, said: kill this man.

Pegasus

The winged horse was real, in the myth. He was born from the blood of Medusa when Perseus cut off her head — which gives him a startling origin for an animal most often depicted in pastoral terms, gleaming white and somewhat serene. Pegasus was drinking at the spring of Pirene in Corinth when Bellerophon found him, and Bellerophon caught him not through strength or cunning but through a dream: Athena appeared to him while he slept on her altar and showed him a golden bridle, and he woke to find the bridle in his hand, and the horse waiting.

This detail matters. The version of the Bellerophon myth that portrays him as purely a man of action misses the devotion that preceded the winged horse. He spent a night alone on the stone floor of Athena's temple, asking for what he couldn't accomplish by himself. The goddess provided. What he later did with what she provided is a different question — but the beginning of the story involves a man who asked, and was answered.

The Labours

What followed was a sequence of tasks that would be recognizable to anyone who knows the myth of Heracles: dangerous missions assigned by a king who expected them to end badly, and was progressively more alarmed when they didn't. Bellerophon killed the Chimera — that improbable hybrid of lion, goat, and serpent that had been terrorising Lycia — using Pegasus to approach from above and a lead-tipped spear that melted in the creature's fire-breath and poured down its throat. He defeated the Solymi warriors. He repelled an Amazon raid. He survived an ambush. At each stage, the king expecting his death had instead to give him more: his daughter, half the kingdom, his reverence.

Pindar's second Olympian ode describes Bellerophon's relationship with the divine as one of particular closeness. The gods were watching. They appear to have been pleased, for a considerable stretch of time, by what they saw.

The Fall

The hubris is real. An old man — famous, beloved by the Lycians, with a winged horse and a kingdom and the regard of the gods — looks at what he has accumulated over a lifetime and decides that it entitles him to the next thing. He points Pegasus toward Olympus.

Zeus sends a gadfly. Pegasus is stung, rears, and Bellerophon falls.

He doesn't die. He lands in the Aleian Field — a plain in Cilicia whose name carries a sense of aimless wandering — blind, lame, and alone, and spends the remainder of his life walking through it, avoiding the paths of men, eating whatever the field provided. The ancient sources disagree on how long this lasted. The poets preferred not to dwell on it. Homer mentions in passing that Bellerophon became hated by the gods and wandered the Aleian plain alone, "eating his heart out." The precision of that phrase, in the translation, is something to pause over.

The fall is not the whole story. It is the part of the story the myth uses as a lesson — about ambition, about the distance between mortal and divine, about what happens when a man who has been favoured by the gods mistakes that favour for permission. But the lesson is only legible against the full account of what preceded it: the false accusation, the years of service, the genuine accomplishments, the long period of divine attention and approval. Bellerophon is not a man who wanted too much from the beginning. He is a man who arrived, by gradual stages, at a point where he could no longer distinguish between what he had earned and what he was owed.
