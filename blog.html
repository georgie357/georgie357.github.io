Who Was Penthesilea? The Amazon Queen History Almost Forgot

The name Penthesilea appears in Homer's Iliad exactly once — a brief, parenthetical mention in the poem's closing lines. The fuller account of her story survived in the Aethiopis, a now-lost continuation of the Trojan War narrative. What those fragments tell us about the last War-Queen of the Amazons is considerably more interesting than what the legend usually remembers.

The Real Myth of Bellerophon: Why He Fell From Heaven

Most people who know the name Bellerophon know two things: he rode a winged horse called Pegasus, and he fell. The simplified version of the myth omits almost everything that makes the story interesting — including how much was taken from Bellerophon before he was given anything, and why the gods, who had been watching him closely for years, chose to draw the line precisely where they did.

Hecuba of Troy: The Queen Behind the War

The Trojan War as Greek mythology tells it is primarily a war about men. Hecuba of Troy does not fit neatly into the story's usual categories. She was Queen of Troy, mother of fifty children, and the most competent person in any room she entered — and Euripides wrote two plays about her. That is more plays than he wrote about Achilles.
