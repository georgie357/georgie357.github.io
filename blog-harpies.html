The Harpies: Wind, Hunger, and the Prophet Who Knew Too Much

He could see everything except what was in front of him. Every time food was set before him, the Harpies came. Zeus had sent them, and Zeus alone could call them off.

The Prophet's Problem

Phineus was a seer — which is to say he was a man who had been given access to the structure of things, to the threads of cause and consequence that the gods preferred to keep to themselves. Ancient sources disagree on the precise nature of his gift: some say Apollo granted it, others that he simply had an unusual facility for reading divine signs. What they agree on is the quality of his knowledge. He was among the most accurate prophets in the ancient world. He could tell you what was going to happen, and he was right.

Zeus found this impolite.

The gods in Greek mythology are not, as a rule, opposed to prophecy. Apollo's oracle at Delphi was a divine institution. Tiresias spoke with Zeus's knowledge. The issue with Phineus was one of degree: he revealed too much, too specifically, to too many mortals. He disclosed knowledge that the gods considered their own property — the shape of things that had not yet happened — and he did so without apparent concern for the boundary he was crossing.

Zeus blinded him. That was the punishment for the eyes that had read too far. Then, because blindness alone seemed insufficient, Zeus sent the Harpies.

What the Harpies Were

The Harpies appear in Hesiod's Theogony as Aello and Ocypete — names that mean "stormwind" and "swift wing" — daughters of the sea-god Thaumas and the oceanid Electra. They were siblings to Iris, the rainbow goddess and messenger of Zeus. In the earliest sources, they are beautiful: Hesiod calls them lovely-haired. They move with the speed of wind because they are, in some sense, made of wind.

By the time of Apollonius of Rhodes — whose Argonautica provides the fullest account of the Phineus episode — the Harpies had acquired a more predatory character. Bird bodies, talons, human faces. The details vary across sources, but the function is consistent: they were fast, they were purposeful, and they were executing divine will. They were not acting from malice. They were doing what Zeus had sent them to do.

The punishment they administered was precise. Every time Phineus sat down to eat, the Harpies arrived. They took the food directly — snatching it from the table and from his hands before he could consume it. What they left behind, they fouled. The smell that remained made it impossible for anyone near the table to eat. The result was a form of starvation administered in plain sight, at a table that was regularly laid with food, in a house where there was no shortage of anything except the ability to finish a meal.

The Argonauts at Salmydessus

When Jason and the Argonauts put in at Salmydessus — a coastal city in Thrace, on the shore of the Black Sea — they found Phineus in the condition Apollonius describes: old, thin from long privation, blind, waiting. He had been living with the Harpies for long enough that the townspeople had stopped trying to help him. There was nothing to be done. The Harpies came whenever he ate. He survived on whatever scraps he could consume in the instant before they arrived.

He had heard enough of prophecy to know the Argonauts were coming. He was waiting specifically for them, because among their number were Zetes and Calais — the Boreads, the winged sons of Boreas the North Wind — and he knew what they could do.

The trap was straightforward. A meal was laid. Phineus sat down. The Boreads positioned themselves. When the Harpies arrived in their usual storm-gust manner — Apollonius compares them to flashes of lightning — the Boreads launched themselves into the air and gave chase.

The Chase and the Oath

The pursuit went across the sea, all the way to the Strophades — small islands in the Ionian sea, off the coast of what is now western Greece. The islands take their name from the myth: strophades means "islands of turning," because this is where Zetes and Calais turned back. Before they could kill the Harpies, the goddess Iris descended from the sky.

Iris was the Harpies' sister. She was also the instrument of Zeus's command. She told the Boreads to stand down — the Harpies were Zeus's creatures, not prey — and swore by the waters of the Styx that the Harpies would never return to Phineus's house. The Styx oath was the most binding in the divine world. Even gods who broke it faced consequences. Iris's oath was a permanent guarantee.

The Harpies were not destroyed. They were recalled. Banished back to wherever they lived when they were not being deployed. They had done what Zeus sent them to do; now they were released from that assignment. The punishment was over not because Phineus had been forgiven but because a different divine actor — Iris — had intervened with sufficient authority to negotiate a binding resolution.

The Meal and What Followed

Phineus ate. Apollonius does not dwell on this. The narrative moves quickly, because there is a lot of ground to cover in the Argonautica and the Harpies are one episode among many. But the image persists: an old blind man, thin from years of administered starvation, sitting at a table with food that stays on it. Eating.

In return, Phineus told the Argonauts what they needed to know. Specifically, he told them how to pass the Symplegades — the Clashing Rocks, the moving islands at the entrance to the Black Sea that had destroyed every ship that had tried to pass between them. He gave them the method, the timing, and the warning. It was the most valuable piece of navigational intelligence available in the ancient world at that moment, and Phineus had been holding it, along with everything else he knew, for a very long time.

He had been saving it up.

The Mechanism of the Punishment

What the Harpies' punishment shares with the other great torments of Greek mythology — Sisyphus's boulder, Tantalus's water, Ixion's wheel — is the principle of perpetual denial. The thing desired is always present and always withheld. Tantalus stands in water that retreats from his lips. Phineus sits before food that is taken from his hands. The proximity is the punishment. If the food were simply absent, the torture would be ordinary hunger. The food is there, the table is laid, and the moment of consumption is perpetually interrupted.

There is an additional element in Phineus's case that the Tartarus punishments do not share: the punishment was administered by creatures who were not his enemies. The Harpies bore him no ill will. They were wind, made purposeful. They came because Zeus sent them, and they would have continued to come until Zeus recalled them or something changed the divine calculus. The impersonality of it — the fact that his torment was not the product of anyone's hatred but of a bureaucratic divine decision, maintained by creatures incapable of malice — is its own kind of horror.

He could see everything, in the old days before Zeus blinded him. He could not see what was in front of him: a meal he would be allowed to finish.

When it finally ended, he did not celebrate. He ate, and then he talked, and the Argonauts went on their way toward the Clashing Rocks. The Strophades remained as a geographical record of where the pursuit had ended. The islands are still there, off the coast of the Peloponnese, smaller than you would expect a place of such consequence to be.
