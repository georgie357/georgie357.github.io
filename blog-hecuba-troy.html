Hecuba of Troy: The Queen Behind the War

On the Hecuba myth, the vengeance at Cynossema, and why Euripides wrote two plays about her

← Back to the blog

The Trojan War as Greek mythology tells it is primarily a war about men. The causes are nominally female — Helen's face, the three goddesses and the golden apple, the judgment of Paris on the slopes of Mount Ida — but the story that emerged from those causes is the story of Achilles and Hector, Odysseus and Agamemnon, Ajax and Diomedes. The women of Troy appear in the narrative as witnesses, mourners, and prizes. They watch from the walls. They weep over the bodies. They are distributed among the victors when it's over.

Hecuba of Troy does not fit neatly into any of these categories.

She was Queen of Troy. In most versions of the Hecuba myth she had fifty children — nineteen by Priam, including Hector and Paris, and others by arrangements the ancient sources didn't always specify. She had married Priam when he was already old. She had watched the city grow around her, watched her sons become what they were, watched ten years of war from the palace walls. She was, in every sense that mattered, the person who kept Troy functioning while the men fought below.

Euripides wrote two plays about her. That is more plays than he wrote about Achilles.

The Myth Before the Myth

Hecuba appears in the background of the Trojan War story well before the fall of Troy. She had a prophetic dream before Paris was born that suggested the child would cause the city's destruction — one of the myths' many moments where prophecy is entirely accurate and entirely ineffective — and she and Priam sent the infant to Mount Ida to die. The shepherd Agelaus didn't follow instructions. He raised Paris instead. Paris eventually returned to Troy, was recognized and reclaimed, went to Sparta, and brought back Helen, because the myth required it.

She had another son, Polydorus, the youngest, who was sent to the court of Polymestor, King of Thrace, for safekeeping when the war's outcome began to look uncertain. Polymestor was Priam's ally. He had been given gold along with the boy.

He killed Polydorus for the gold.

The Night Troy Fell

When Troy fell, Hecuba lost almost everything she had in the space of one night. Priam was killed at the altar of Zeus Herkeios — the protector of the household, which has its own particular irony — by Neoptolemus, Achilles's son. Hector was already dead, dragged behind Achilles's chariot for twelve days while Hecuba watched from the walls. Her daughter Polyxena was sacrificed on Achilles's tomb. Her prophetic daughter Cassandra, who had been right about everything since Apollo cursed her, was taken as a prize by Agamemnon.

Hecuba herself was assigned as a slave to Odysseus. He is short and wiry, the myth tells us, and his vanity is intellectual rather than physical, which is perhaps the most specific kind.

The Vengeance at Cynossema

The second half of the Hecuba myth takes place in Thrace, on the shores near Polymestor's kingdom. It is there that she discovers Polydorus — fourteen years old, wearing a blue tunic, his body washed up on the shore. It is there that she constructs her vengeance: inviting Polymestor and his sons into her tent on the pretext of showing him where Priam had hidden gold, and blinding Polymestor with the brooches from her Phrygian mother's jewellery. Euripides presents this act with a clarity that doesn't flinch from what it is, while making it entirely understandable. The law Hecuba is operating by is older than the laws of the Greeks. Polymestor killed a child he had been trusted to protect. The response is proportionate.

The transformation happens at Cynossema — the headland on the Thracian coast. In Euripides, Polymestor prophesies it: Hecuba will become a dog with fiery eyes, will leap into the sea, will haunt the headland. The headland keeps the name Cynossema — the Dog's Tomb — for centuries after, in the accounts of sailors who passed it without knowing quite why it had that name.

Why Hecuba?

Euripides wrote about Hecuba because she presented a problem the Greek tragic tradition found productive: a person of genuine dignity, genuine authority, and genuine moral standing who is subjected to losses so extreme and so cumulative that what emerges at the end is something that can no longer be contained by the forms of civilized life she had spent fifty years constructing.

She is not a victim in the sense that the word implies passivity. She plans. She acts. She achieves her object. She is not a monster in the sense that the word implies something alien. Everything she does follows from premises the audience can follow and — Euripides makes clear — understand. She is a woman at the absolute limit of what a person can endure and remain, in any meaningful sense, themselves.

The transformation is not a punishment. It is the only authentic state left available to her.
